## WRITING DNA — these 10 rules shape every decision below:

1. **Answer first.** The article must answer the main question within the first 50 words. No background, no scene-setting, no "In today's world..." — the answer comes first.
2. **Question chain.** Structure the entire article as a sequence of questions the reader will naturally ask next. Each H2 is a question the previous section raised. The reader should never wonder "why am I reading this section now?"
3. **One idea per paragraph.** Never combine two points in one block. If a paragraph covers two things, split it.
4. **Example every time.** No abstract explanation without a concrete case. If you state a rule, show it in action immediately. If you describe a mistake, show the broken output. If you describe a fix, show the corrected output.
5. **8th grade reading level.** Short sentences. Simple words. If a simpler word exists, use it.
6. **No filler intro.** Start with the answer, not the background. The first sentence should be the most useful sentence in the article.
7. **H2s are questions or promises.** Every H2 should be a question the reader is thinking, or a direct promise like "How to fix X" / "Why X breaks Y". Never use label-style headings like "Overview" or "Background."
8. **Pull-forward endings.** End each section with a one-line takeaway or transition that pulls the reader into the next section. The reader should feel compelled to keep reading, not feel like they reached a stopping point.
9. **Define on contact.** If you use a term the reader might not know, explain it immediately in the next sentence. Not in a glossary. Not later. Right there.
10. **Read-aloud test.** Before publishing, read aloud. If you pause awkwardly, rewrite that sentence.

These rules are not suggestions. They override every other instinct. When the skeleton instructions below say "write X," it means "write X while obeying all 10 rules."

## YOUR TASK — produce the following in order:

### 1. MISTAKE MINING
Identify 5–7 concrete mistakes people make related to this topic. For each, note:
- What the mistake looks like in practice (show a real example of the broken output or wrong approach, not a vague description — Rule 4)
- Why people make it (the wrong mental model behind it — name it clearly so a reader with no background understands it — Rule 9)
- How damaging it is (minor annoyance vs. fundamentally broken result)

### 2. SELECT THE BEST 3–5 MISTAKES
Pick the mistakes that:
- Are genuinely common (not edge cases only experts encounter)
- Have a clear, teachable fix (not "it depends")
- Build on each other as a question chain: fixing mistake 1 naturally leads the reader to ask "but what about...?" which is mistake 2 (Rule 2)
- Cover enough ground to make the article feel complete, not repetitive

Order them so each mistake is the natural next question after the previous fix.

### 3. KEYWORD STRATEGY
Propose:
- **Primary keyphrase**: The exact phrase this article should rank for. Choose based on search relevance and natural fit.
- **Secondary keyphrases** (2–3): Supporting phrases that reinforce topical authority
- **Cannibalization check**: Confirm why this keyphrase does NOT compete with other likely site pages
- **Search intent label**: Is this informational, investigational, or educational?

### 4. UNIQUE ANGLE
Write 2–3 sentences describing why this article is not just another "top N mistakes" listicle. What pattern connects the mistakes? What single root cause do most of them share? This connective thread is what makes the article worth reading start to finish, not just skimming one section.

### 5. ARTICLE SKELETON

**Proposed Title** (rankable, specific, not clickbait — should signal "mistakes" or "fixes" or "wrong")
**Meta Description** (under 155 characters, includes primary keyphrase)

**Introduction outline:**
- First sentence: directly answer the main question or state the core fix in under 50 words (Rule 1, Rule 6). Not "many people struggle with..." — the answer itself.
- Second sentence: the one root cause behind most of the mistakes (this is the hook)
- Third sentence: what the reader will be able to do differently after reading
- No background. No context-setting. The reader already knows the topic — they searched for it.

**Section-by-section outline:**
For each mistake/fix section provide:
- H2 heading: framed as the question the reader is now asking, or a "Why X breaks Y" promise (Rule 7). The H2 from section N+1 must feel like the natural follow-up to section N's takeaway (Rule 2).
- The mistake: what it looks like, with a concrete BEFORE example. One idea only (Rule 3). Show the broken output, not just describe it (Rule 4).
- Why it happens: the wrong assumption or mental model, named simply. If you use a technical term, define it in the same sentence (Rule 9).
- The fix: the corrected approach, with a concrete AFTER example placed directly after the fix explanation (Rule 4).
- Pull-forward line: one sentence that transitions to the next mistake. This should raise the question that the next H2 answers (Rule 8, Rule 2).
- Internal link opportunity (where relevant)

**"What ties these mistakes together?" section:**
- H2 is a question (Rule 7)
- The shared root cause or mental model shift, stated plainly (Rule 5)
- One concrete example that shows the mental model shift in action (Rule 4)
- Pull-forward line to the checklist conclusion (Rule 8)

**Conclusion outline:**
- Quick-reference checklist of all fixes (scannable, no new information, one idea per line — Rule 3)
- CTA: natural transition to the course or related content

### 6. CONTENT DEPTH SIGNALS
List 5–8 specific things this article must include to signal depth:
- At least 3 concrete before/after example pairs (Rule 4 — no exceptions)
- A named wrong mental model that causes most of the mistakes (defined immediately when introduced — Rule 9)
- A quick-reference fix checklist readers can screenshot or bookmark
- A real scenario where the mistake causes a visibly bad outcome (shown, not described — Rule 4)
- A contrast between what beginners do vs. what practitioners do (with examples of both — Rule 4)
- Any relevant pattern or observation worth citing

## CONSTRAINTS:
- Every mistake must have a BEFORE (broken) and AFTER (fixed) example. No exceptions. The examples must be concrete and specific, not abstract descriptions. (Rule 4)
- Do not pad with obvious mistakes just to hit a number. Three genuine mistakes are better than five where two are filler.
- The tone is direct, helpful, and practitioner-friendly. Not condescending, not academic. (Rule 5)
- Each H2 must answer a question the reader would actually search for or ask. (Rule 7)
- The H2 sequence must read as a conversation: each one is the next thing the reader wants to know after finishing the previous section. (Rule 2)
- The article must be highly on-page SEO optimized: primary keyphrase in the title, first paragraph, at least one H2, and meta description, with secondary keyphrases distributed naturally.
- Do NOT write generic advice like "practice more" or "be specific." Every fix must be an actionable instruction the reader can apply in their next attempt.
- No paragraph may contain more than one idea. (Rule 3)
- Every section must end with a pull-forward line. (Rule 8)
- The first sentence of the article must contain the answer, not background. (Rule 1, Rule 6)
